title: On Grief, and rumination
date: 2026-05-23
---
Grief and negative rumination look very similar from the outside. They are almost identical to an audience without the context. Both involve sustained, painful attention to something difficult. Both can pull a person inward — quieter, sadder, harder to reach.
But the experience is different, and the texture of feelings each one leaves you with is very different. Negative rumination is like a loop. You go on and on about something, circling without resolution, and you end up with even more heavy feelings than you started with. It doesn't help you process; it grinds. 
Grief, on the other hand, comes in waves. It comes and goes like phases of the moon — one day you're feeling it a lot more, and the next it isn't as present. 
And because the two can look so similar from the outside, it's easy to misjudge a grieving person as someone who's just ruminating — especially since so many people around us are ruminating about so many things. But whether someone is grieving or ruminating, the response that actually helps is the same: listening. Lending an ear. Asking someone not to grieve, or not to ruminate, is unkind. Telling them to "just be grateful," or "just be okay," closes a door. It might feel like advice, but to the person on the receiving end it lands as dismissal — as if their pain is something to be tidied up for the comforter's sake. All of us know, very well, how brutal it feels to be dismissed about anything. So even when it's exhausting to keep listening — and it can be exhausting, because they may end up venting a lot more to you — that's still the more useful thing to offer. Advice is rarely what they need. Being heard is.
Grief, after all, is a response to a loss. The loss could be an identity, a relationship, a future, or a self. Every loss in life deserves to be grieved through. And grief isn't a tidy process — it's slow, it comes in waves, it's not linear, it's not one-dimensional. It doesn't simply go away, either. You, as a person, expand around it. It softens over time, but it doesn't disappear.
Part of what makes the difference between grief and rumination is how much agency and self-awareness you bring to your own inner life — how much responsibility you hold for what's happening to you and within you. Rumination tends to thrive in passivity, in being swept along by your own thoughts. Grief, even when it overwhelms you, can be moved through — but only if you stay in some contact with it, rather than just circling around it.
The funny thing is that gratitude does arise — as part of processing grief, not as a substitute for it. In fact, it's the very attachment and love that brings grief in the first place. You don't grieve what didn't matter. Grief is a shadow cast by love or meaning. An ache for something that mattered.
