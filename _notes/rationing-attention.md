Once you shift the frame from time to [attention](https://rohanmahnot.space/notes/seize-the-day/), things become clear.

Everyone gets access to the same amount of time, 24 hours in a day. But the attention varies. Some days a single conversation leaves me depleted. Other days I work for hours and can still feel sharp. I’ve meditated over it, for a while to understand it’s where I’m spending my attention. 

The question I keep coming back to - what is actually consuming my attention, and do I even know?

## Mapping Where Attention Goes

I’m going to talk about [attention as finite resource](https://rohanmahnot.space/notes/on-attention-2/), like a currency. Every attention expenditure is not visible. Some is planned like a work project, or a difficult conversation you’ve prepared for. Some is unplanned but still visible like a production issue, or a falling sick. You know it’s costing you, even if you didn’t plan for it in advance.

But then there’s another category that’s invisible. Attention you spend without knowing it. This is where a lot of leaks are, which end up costing you. And because you can’t see them, you can’t manage them.

Awareness, then, is a foundational tool, since everything else depends on it.

### 1. The Invisible Drains

Large expenditures have clear causality. After a brainstorming session or an intense analytical discussion, you know why you’re tired. After a fight with your partner, you know why you feel hollow. The cost and the cause are both visible.

But a lot of life is made of micro-expenditures. Ambient noise on a commute, a quick scroll, a moment of low-level social friction. Each of them, individually seems negligible. However, they accumulate, and lead to exhaustion. Since the individual costs are small, we never connect them to our depletion. We just feel drained and can’t explain why.

I find Kahneman’s System 1 / System 2 framework useful to understand here. 
System 2 - intentional, effortful thinking. You know when you’ve used, and why. 
System 1 - automatic, associative processing. Operates mostly below awareness. 
Its costs are invisible. The emotional texture of an environment, ambient stimulation, the micro-decisions of daily life — all draw from the same pool, while appearing with no obvious costs.

### 2. Working Memory Load

Another invisible drain is how much you’re carrying in your head. When you haven’t offloaded your thoughts your mind holds onto them. A full working memory impairs clear thinking because of the cumulative load.

I’ve noticed this with myself, the more regularly I journal, the more clearly I think, and the speed of decisions goes up. 

But there’s an open question I sit with here — does dumping my thoughts help because it releases the emotional weight, freeing up the attention tied to unresolved feeling? Or is it simply the act of moving things from your head to an external surface that creates the relief? 
Its probably both, but they’re very different mechanisms. Recharging depleted vsis different from freeing up occupied attention.

### 3. The Gut-Mind Conflict

A subtler drain, is the conflict between gut feeling and reasoned conclusion. When the two don’t agree, the mind keeps passively returning to it, checking whether it’s been settled. 

It’s the unresolved nature of the disagreement, which ends up draining. The resolution doesn’t always require settling the conflict. Sometimes just naming it explicitly, and acknowledging can be enough to reduce the attention burn. 
What costs you is the unacknowledged loop, not the tension itself.

## How to Preserve Attention
This works at three levels: *visibility*, *prevention*, and *recovery*.

1. **Visibility**
    You can’t manage what you can’t see. Building a habit of asking — what is actually consuming my attention right now? — is where it starts. Journaling and honest audits of your environments are good tools to help with this this. The goal is to surface the invisible.
    
2. **Prevention**
    1. Limit exposure to low-ROI attention sinks. A noisy commute, passive scrolling, meetings that didn’t need to happen — expenditures with poor returns. 
    2. Reduce context-switching. Every switch between types of thinking carries a transition cost. Batching similar work and limiting interruptions within a task reduces this overhead significantly.
    3. [Offload your cognitive load](https://rohanmahnot.space/notes/pkms/). Working memory is finite. Externalise whatever you can — open decisions, tasks, worries et. The goal isn’t organisation for its own sake; it’s keeping working memory available for what actually needs it.
    4. Close open loops. Unresolved things like deferred decisions, unaddressed conflicts et. occupy mental bandwidth passively. Closing a loop doesn’t mean resolving it fully. Sometimes it just means deciding consciously when you’ll return to it. 
    The mind needs to know something is handled, not necessarily solved.
    5. Manage the depth of engagement. Even when you can’t limit exposure, you can choose how much of yourself to bring to something. The trade off to consider is Depth of engagement and the frequency.
3. **Recovery**
    Attention also needs to be replenished, not just rationed. Undirected, low-demand experience, like a walk, time in nature, unstructured rest et. help with recovery. This is different from entertainment, which still demands engagement. And sleep is one of foundational one.
